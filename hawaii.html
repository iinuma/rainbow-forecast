💡What is this?

Hawaii is called the Rainbow State for a reason — the Ko'olau mountains force warm trade winds upward, creating rain on one side of the island while the sun shines on the other. Over 200 rainbow days a year in areas like Kailua and Kāne'ohe.

But rainbows aren't in weather forecasts. This map fills that gap. It checks the actual conditions — sun angle, cloud cover, and rainfall — across Oahu every 10 minutes and scores each area from 0 to 100.

It won't guarantee a rainbow. But when the score is high, it's worth stepping outside.

🔍How does it work?

Four conditions are checked to calculate each area's rainbow score.

☀️
Sun Angle
Rainbows appear when the sun is between 0° and 42° above the horizon — morning and late afternoon are prime time.
☁️
Cloud Cover
If clouds block the sun, no rainbow. We check that cloud cover is under 70% on the sunny side.
🌧️
Rain Nearby
You need raindrops to split the sunlight. Live precipitation data confirms whether rain is present in the area.
🧭
Direction Guide
Rainbows appear opposite the sun. The map header shows which direction to look based on the current sun position.

🔬Why Sun + Rain = Rainbow

A rainbow forms when sunlight enters a raindrop, reflects inside it, and exits at an angle of about 42° toward your eyes. That's why the sun must be behind you and the rain in front of you — and why the sun's altitude matters so much.

❓FAQ

The score was high but I didn't see a rainbow
Weather data comes from numerical models, which don't capture every local micro-condition. Think of a high score as "conditions look favorable" — not a guarantee. Rainbows are still magic.
What area does this cover?
The map covers roughly a 70km × 70km area centered near Pearl City, including Honolulu, Waikiki, Diamond Head, Kailua, Kāne'ohe, North Shore, and most of Oahu's major areas.
How often does it update?
Every 10 minutes. The map skips nighttime and midday hours automatically, since rainbows can't form when the sun is too high (above 42°) or below the horizon. Best viewing windows are roughly 6–9 AM and 3–6 PM HST.
Do I need to install anything?
Nope. It's just a webpage. Works on any smartphone or browser, no account needed.
Why does Hawaii have so many rainbows?
The Ko'olau mountain range intercepts the northeast trade winds, pushing moist air upward and creating localized rain on the windward side — while the leeward side stays sunny. That combination of rain + sun at the same time is exactly what makes a rainbow. Areas like Kailua and Kāne'ohe see over 200 rainbow days a year.
